Three Years Later: How CMS's Risk Adjustment Changes Played Out — And What the 2027 Proposals Mean for Value-Based Care

By Andrew Smithson | TRYNYTY health:enablement ·April 2026 ·15 min read

Three years ago, when CMS released the 2024 Advance Notice proposing sweeping changes to the CMS-HCC risk adjustment model, I wrote a piece arguing that those revisions were contradictory to the agency’s own goals of advancing health equity, innovation, and affordability. I warned that flattening coefficients for chronic conditions like diabetes and removing nuance from the model would reverse a decade of progress in reducing health inequities, disincentivize early detection and preventive care, and ultimately drive up costs by harming value-based provider organizations.

At the time, my concerns might have seemed hyperbolic — CMS touted the changes as a way to curb fraud and align payments with value. But the data tells a different story.

Fast forward to 2026: a Milliman study revealed that roughly 10% of Medicare Advantage enrollees — approximately 3.4 million people — were forced to switch plans due to exits, mergers, or drastic benefit cuts.1 Premiums rose an average of 5.2%, out-of-pocket maximums climbed 3–4%, and networks narrowed, disproportionately hitting low-income, rural, and minority beneficiaries.2 High-performing providers in value-based arrangements faced revenue drops of 20–35%.

These outcomes aligned closely with my predictions. The one piece I got wrong was the timeline — I argued the changes might save the Medicare Trust Fund in the short term but would accelerate its depletion over time. In reality, the cost increases were more immediate, pushing insolvency projections to 2033 — three years earlier than pre-2024 estimates.3

If those warnings proved prescient, then we should pay close attention now. CMS’s recently released 2027 Advance Notice does more to exacerbate the flaws in the V28 model than to correct them. This piece revisits what went wrong with the original changes, what the real-world fallout looks like, and what the 2027 proposals mean for ACOs and risk-bearing providers.

What Went Wrong With the V28 Changes

The core problem was that CMS misidentified correlation as causation. Their assessment found certain chronic conditions were documented at higher rates in Medicare Advantage than in traditional Fee-for-Service Medicare — and concluded this reflected discretionary or fraudulent coding. It didn’t.

Medicare Advantage populations are disproportionately socioeconomically challenged, with higher rates of chronic illness. They’re enrolled in managed care models where care coordination, proactive outreach, and preventive services are standard — all of which result in more complete and accurate documentation of patient risk. Traditional Medicare beneficiaries are more likely to see providers operating under volume-based FFS incentives, with less emphasis on comprehensive risk capture.

What CMS interpreted as excess coding in MA was actually more complete coding — a feature, not a bug, of value-based care models. By removing over 2,000 diagnosis codes under “Principle 10” and flattening coefficient hierarchies for conditions like diabetes, CMS simultaneously penalized the providers doing the best work and violated its own “Principle 5,” which calls for specificity in diagnostic classification.

The Real-World Fallout: 2024–2026

The impacts confirmed what critics predicted. Analysis from America’s Physician Groups documented revenue impacts shifting from a proposed –3.12% risk model effect to –2.16% after modifications — still insufficient to prevent the plan exits and benefit reductions that followed.5 MA enrollment growth slowed materially post-2024, with drops among Black and Hispanic beneficiaries reversing equity gains documented in CMS’s own research.

High-performing provider groups — physician-managed organizations and ACOs investing in preventive care and chronic disease management — saw revenues plummet as flattened coefficients failed to capture the nuance of their patient populations. The financial pressure forced contract renegotiations, network contractions, and in some cases, exits from MA markets entirely.

How the 2027 Proposals Build on the Same Foundation

The 2027 Advance Notice maintains V28’s 86 HCCs and hierarchies, recalibrating coefficients using more recent post-COVID data. The net effect: a –3.32% risk score impact overall — a further reduction on top of the already-reduced V28 baseline.

Some elements of the 2027 proposal are genuinely positive. Excluding unlinked chart reviews and audio-only encounters from risk scores improves data integrity and ties payments more directly to actual care — a reasonable reform. But these improvements don’t address the structural problem: a coefficient hierarchy that continues to undervalue chronic disease management while assigning outsized weights to advanced, often hospital-treated complications.

The pattern is clear. Conditions like depression and diabetes — where proactive management prevents far more costly downstream events — see continued coefficient reductions. Meanwhile, the severe complications that result from inadequate chronic disease management remain highly compensated. The model rewards escalation over stabilization.

“Such models should not penalize effective treatment or preventive care.” — American College of Physicians, comments on CY 2027 proposals6

What This Means for ACOs and Risk-Bearing Providers

Risk score trajectory: If your attributed population includes high proportions of patients with well-managed chronic conditions — which is precisely the goal of value-based care — your prospective risk scores will continue to understate true patient complexity. This affects benchmark calculations in MSSP and capitation adequacy in Medicare Advantage.

Contract strategy: Organizations with Medicare Advantage risk contracts should stress-test their financial models against the 2027 coefficients now, before contract renewal cycles. The –3.32% risk score impact compounds across populations and could significantly affect per-member payment adequacy.

Documentation and coding: Comprehensive documentation remains critical — both for the conditions still captured in the model and for the SDoH data that may influence future model revisions. Accurate risk capture protects against audit findings and positions organizations well for potential improvements.

Advocacy: The public comment period represents the most direct lever organizations have. America’s Physician Groups, the American College of Physicians, and the American Hospital Association all filed substantive comments on the 2027 proposals. Provider organizations and ACOs should participate — directly or through their trade associations.

A Path Forward

The solution isn’t to abandon risk adjustment — it’s to design it more carefully. Expand the model to incorporate SDoH ICD-10 codes, which would better predict future costs and incentivize documenting and addressing the barriers that drive health inequities. Restore nuance to chronic condition coefficients. Implement significant changes with phased timelines and robust stakeholder input.

As the 2024–2026 experience demonstrated, ignoring causation in policy design has real consequences for real patients. The 2027 proposals offer an opportunity to course-correct. Whether CMS takes it remains to be seen — but provider organizations shouldn’t wait to find out.
