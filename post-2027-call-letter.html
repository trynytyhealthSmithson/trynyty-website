CMS's 2027 Medicare Advantage Proposals: Doubling Down on a Model That Punishes Prevention and Rewards Sick Care

By Andrew Smithson | TRYNYTY health:enablement · April 2025 · 15 min read

Three years ago, when CMS released the 2024 Advance Notice proposing sweeping changes to the CMS-HCC risk adjustment model, I wrote a piece arguing that those revisions were contradictory to the agency's own goals of advancing health equity, innovation, and affordability. I warned that flattening coefficients for chronic conditions like diabetes and removing nuance from the model would reverse a decade of progress in reducing health inequities, disincentivize early detection and preventive care, and ultimately drive up costs by harming value-based provider organizations.

At the time, my concerns might have seemed hyperbolic — CMS touted the changes as a way to curb fraud and align payments with value. But the data tells a different story.

Fast forward to 2026: a Milliman study revealed that about 10% of Medicare Advantage enrollees — roughly 3.4 million people — were forced to switch plans due to exits, mergers, or drastic benefit cuts. Premiums rose an average of 5.2%, out-of-pocket maximums climbed 3–4%, and networks narrowed, disproportionately hitting low-income, rural, and minority beneficiaries. High-performing providers in value-based arrangements faced revenue drops of 20–35%.

If those warnings proved prescient, then we should pay close attention now. CMS's recently released 2027 Advance Notice does more to exacerbate the flaws in the V28 model than to correct them.
